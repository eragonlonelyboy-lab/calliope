# The seven laws of web design (strategy layer)

Load for any website or landing-page engagement at Phase 1 to 2, before direction work begins, and again at the gate. The core bar judges craft; these laws judge whether the site will convert and survive. They are laws, not rules: rules (font pairings, button colors, headline lengths) expire every season; laws are why the rules exist. Distilled from a veteran agency strategy briefing (750+ delivered sites, Fortune 500 roster) supplied 2026-07-10, adapted to CALLIOPE's phases.

## Law I. One job (singularity of purpose)

Every page exists to push a single decision. Competing CTAs fracture the journey and hand the visitor the option to do nothing. The one job comes from the business model: e-commerce = add to cart; consulting = book a call; agency = start a conversation. Multiple buttons may exist, but all loop toward the one job; secondary actions drop visual weight (underlines, not solid buttons).
- **Phase wiring:** the one job is a Phase 1 interview output. If the client cannot name it in one sentence, the interview is not done (non-negotiable 9 does not apply until this question is answered; it is load-bearing).
- **Gate check:** count the competing solid-button CTAs per page. More than one distinct job visible in a viewport is a finding.

## Law II. Categorical clarity (the 30-second test)

A visitor must grasp what the company does and who it is for within 30 seconds, or they leave. Copy defines the category, not the feature: "the first email-only contract revision agent" beats "custom contract provisions in your inbox in 15 minutes." Pair the category claim with adjacent social proof so understanding flows into action.
- **Gate check:** show the hero to someone with zero context for 30 seconds. If they cannot say what it is and who it is for, finding.

## Law III. The first scroll decides the session

The hero must answer four questions before any scroll: Where am I? What do I get? Why should I care? What is the next step? And the next step must be the most frictionless possible; a form in the hero beat a click-away contact page 5:1 in the source's own case (2 inquiries a day to 10).
- **Gate check:** the four questions, answered from the rendered first viewport alone. Any unanswered question is a finding.

## Law IV. Words sell; design makes them land (copy before design)

Copy precedes design in every professional build. The structural hierarchy: the Promise (headline) captures, the Context (subheading) earns the CTA, the Action executes the one job, the Trust (proof) validates. A stunning site with weak copy is a liability.
- **Phase wiring:** in production, the copy pass is sequenced before layout; the Art Director serves the words, never the reverse.
- **Gate check:** the hand test. Cover the design and read only the text; if the offer does not land, copy is the bottleneck and no visual fix is accepted for it.

## Law V. Premium is removal (restraint)

"Premium equals more" is the amateur tell. Authority is elements earning their place: white space, full-bleed imagery, four nav items instead of nine, one deliberate line of text beside strong imagery. This law extends the core bar's restraint dimension from color to everything.
- **Gate check:** the remove-three test. If three elements can leave a section without losing meaning, the section is fighting itself; finding.

## Law VI. Speed and findability are the floor, not an upgrade

A perfect design that loads slowly or never surfaces in search (or, now, in AI-generated answers: AIO) is moot because the visitor never arrives. Performance and findability are structural minimums beneath every other law.
- **Gate check:** already instrumented in the audit loop (load time, semantic HTML, meta). Add: does the site state what it is in machine-readable form an answer engine can cite?

## Law VII. A website is a system, not a project

One-and-done is a recipe for decay: offers evolve, proof points age, integrations break, platforms change rendering. The engagement's final deliverable includes the maintenance frame, not just the launch.
- **Phase wiring:** the handoff pack names what must be revisited and on what cadence (offers, testimonials, integrations, platform rendering). A launch without this page is incomplete.

## The five-question site audit (diagnostics; also the redesign intake)

1. What is the one job of this site? Cannot be answered in one sentence = no conversion path.
2. Does the first scroll answer the clarity questions immediately? A stranger who has to guess will bounce.
3. Are the words selling, or is design masking weak copy? Hand test: cover the design, read the text.
4. Does it feel premium through restraint or cheap through clutter? Remove-three test per section.
5. Is the foundation fast, findable, and maintained as a system? Failing AI answers or slow loads is a leaking foundation design cannot save.

Run all five on any redesign intake before proposing direction, and again as gate questions on every website verdict.
